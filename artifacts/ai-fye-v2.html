AIX Explorers: A First-Year AI Experience

Pilot Program Proposal  ·  SUNY Polytechnic Institute  ·  AIX Center  ·  Spring 2026 → Fall 2026

The AIX Center proposes a selective first-year cohort program — AIX Explorers — built around a 4-credit first-semester curriculum, a named cohort identity, dedicated infrastructure access, and integration into AIX Center research and community. A second-semester continuation is described below. The program pilots in Fall 2026 with 24 students and is designed as the foundation for a first-year Micro-Credential in AI in a subsequent year.

Curriculum

Fall Semester — 4 Credits

FYS 111 · 1 Credit · Graded · Existing Course Number
Living with AI
The fall first-year seminar for AIX Explorers, running alongside STS 190. Two hours per week. Students build the workbench, develop practical campus literacy (time management, library, AI policy mapping across syllabi), and acquire the technical skills STS 190 requires: generative AI operation, agentic tools, local models. The Learner's Permit scaffold runs through FYS 111; completion of Exercise 15 earns the Provisional License.
STS 190 · 3 Credits · Graded · Special Topics · Social Sciences GE
AI and the Human Future
A special topics course examining AI as a social, political, and historical phenomenon. Students read across disciplines and use AI systems as both subject and method throughout. Proposed for Social Sciences general education designation on an expedited basis for this section.

Spring Semester — Continuation

FYS 112 · 1 Credit · Proposal Forthcoming
Living with AI, continued
A spring continuation course maintaining cohort coherence and AI literacy development across the second semester.
TBD · 3 Credits · Humanities or Arts GE
Second Disciplinary Course
A second disciplinary course extending the intellectual work of STS 190 into a different mode. To be determined.

Cohort

Size & Recruitment
24 students. Recruited through Admissions — the program can be described to incoming students as part of that process as soon as useful. Courses held from the schedule until the first-year registration window opens in May.
Selection
No technical background assumed or preferred. Intellectual curiosity across disciplines is the selection criterion.
Identity
AIX Explorers is a named cohort. Cohort membership becomes a transcript notation when the program formalizes as a micro-credential. In the pilot year, it is a program identity.
Living / Learning
Residential Life may find an AIX Explorers living/learning community a natural fit — a designated floor or wing. AIX is well positioned to engage with that conversation. Whether AIX faculty proximity on the same floor is an asset or too close is worth discussing with Res Life.
Senior AI Fellows
Juniors and seniors in Hilltop serve as near-peer mentors — an aspirational upper track visible to Explorers from orientation.
AI Club
Two students are already moving toward launching an AI Club. AIX Explorers feeds directly into that effort, giving the club a defined first-year constituency from the start.

What Students Get

Premium model subscriptions and local model cloud access for the duration of enrollment. Google AI Essentials and Prompting Essentials certificates embedded in the curriculum. A digital badge stack — Provisional License, Google certificates, AIX Explorer membership — visible from semester one. Access to external researchers, practitioners, and policymakers through AIX-organized sessions. Opportunity to serve as beta testers for AIX OER materials in development. Option to produce a student podcast under the Living with AI umbrella.

Each student carries one longitudinal research thread tied to an active AIX project. LP transcripts, with student consent, become primary research data. Where student work contributes to OER materials, students are credited as co-authors. An arms-length external researcher documents the cohort independently.

Paper of the week, failure documentation, a no-AI project, and civil discourse simulation are woven into the course and cohort experience rather than treated as discrete program requirements.

End-of-semester showcase, December 2026. Monthly demo days throughout the year.

STS 190: AI and the Human Future

Special Topics  ·  Social Sciences General Education  ·  3 Credits  ·  AIX Explorers Section  ·  Enrollment cap: 24 (parallel section possible; combined capacity 48)

The cloud is not a cloud. It is submarine cables carrying more than 95% of global communication, data centers consuming land and water and electricity, lithium pulled from the Atacama Desert, tin extracted from Bangka Island, satellites, server farms, and e-waste. Behind every swipe and prompt lies a vast material infrastructure that shapes how we live, communicate, and govern — and that most people never see.

This course uses a single exhibition as its intellectual anchor: City in the Cloud: Data on the Ground — The Architecture of Data (Pinakothek der Moderne, Munich, 2025–2026), organized around three themes (Elemental, Spatial, Temporal) and four guiding questions about the material reality of data infrastructure. In the second half, the course turns from what AI is made of to what it makes of us: consciousness, authorship, rights, democracy, flourishing, and relationship. Together, the two halves constitute the question the course cannot answer but will not stop asking: what is the human future when AI is part of it?

Students engage both halves using AI systems to read, think, and write throughout. Every analytical task is also a literacy practice. Every transcript is a course document. In the second half, students extend into agentic AI operation and work with local models running without cloud infrastructure.

Goals

1
Apply social science analytical capacity to AI as a material, political, and historical system
2
Build sustained, documented practice in reading, thinking, and writing with generative AI as cognitive infrastructure
3
Cultivate the critical capacity to evaluate AI — its claims, its costs, its social consequences — with rigor and from multiple disciplinary angles
4
Engage the open questions of AI's human future (consciousness, rights, democracy, flourishing, relationship) as genuine intellectual problems that do not yet have settled answers

Objectives

1
Demonstrate awareness of social science methods (quantitative and qualitative analysis, policy analysis, technology assessment, evaluation research) and begin to apply these methods as tools to analyze specific AI phenomena: extraction economies, urban data governance, digital archives, algorithmic persuasion, AI consciousness and rights, the future of democracy, human flourishing, and AI companionship
2
Demonstrate reading, thinking, and writing with large language models across a sustained and documented body of analytical work, with transcripts as primary evidence
3
Evaluate sources and arguments about AI — corporate, journalistic, academic, and policy — with awareness of authority, interest, framing, and methodological limitation
4
Operate agentic AI tools for defined research and monitoring tasks, and articulate the design decisions, constraints, and ethical implications involved in deploying AI agents

Student Learning Outcomes

Social Sciences — General Education
Students will describe major concepts and analytical frameworks relevant to AI as a social phenomenon. They will demonstrate awareness of social science methods (qualitative analysis, quantitative analysis, policy analysis, technology assessment, and program evaluation) and begin to apply these methods to AI systems and infrastructures. Students will use these tools to examine specific issues: extraction and environmental impact, urban governance, labor conditions, archival politics, algorithmic influence, AI rights, democratic governance, human flourishing, and the social effects of AI companionship.
Information Literacy — General Education
Students will locate and evaluate information about AI using tools appropriate to the discipline — including AI systems themselves — with awareness of authority, validity, bias, and origin. They will demonstrate understanding of the ethical dimensions of information use, creation, and dissemination in an AI-mediated environment, including questions of data retention, deletion, consent, access, and the politics of memory in digital archives.

Primary Texts

Exhibition — Primary Corpus
City in the Cloud: Data on the Ground
The Architecture of Data · Pinakothek der Moderne / Architekturmuseum der TUM, Munich, 2025–2026. Curator: Damjan Kokalevski · Research Advisor: Marina Otero Verzier. Exhibition brochure, catalogue, and Data Talks podcast series (seven episodes).
Supplementary Text
Atlas of AI
Kate Crawford · Yale University Press, 2021. Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence. Selected chapters assigned alongside exhibition materials.

Weekly Arc

Part One — What Is AI Made Of? · Weeks 1–6 · The material, extractive, and spatial reality of data infrastructure
WEEK 01
The Cloud Is Not a Cloud
Introduction to the course, the exhibition, and the RTW method. Students set up workbenches, begin first AI conversations about data infrastructure, and export their first transcripts. Crawford, Introduction.
WEEK 02
What Is the Cloud Made Of?
Submarine cables, data centers, satellites. Material history of global communication infrastructure. Geopolitical and colonial entanglements. Exhibition: Elemental chapter. Data Talks Ep. 1: Data Centers.
WEEK 03
Is Data the New Currency?
Lithium mining in the Atacama Desert. Tin extraction on Bangka Island. Struggle for water rights. Environmental and community costs of lithium, copper, cobalt, tin. Exhibition: Critical Resources. Crawford, Ch. 1 (Earth).
WEEK 04
Is the City More Than a Computer?
The smart city as optimization engine. Surveillance capitalism versus data as public infrastructure. Munich Digital Twin as case study. Exhibition: Spatial chapter. Data Talks Ep. 6: Urban Data Governance.
WEEK 05
To Keep or Let Go of Data?
Data retention as political act. Digital archives and historical memory. AI training, bias, and what gets preserved or erased. E-waste and the infrastructure of forgetting. Exhibition: Temporal chapter. Data Talks Ep. 7.
WEEK 06
Synthesis: Reading the Material Half
Students upload five weeks of transcripts and artifacts. Using AI, they produce a synthesis document across the corpus. Discussion: what does it mean to use AI to analyze the infrastructure AI runs on?
Transition — From Material to Human · Weeks 7–8 · Extending the method; introducing agentic tools
WEEK 07
What Does AI Make of Us?
Pivot from material to human future. Introduction to the six debate questions. Students choose positions, identify evidence needed, begin building information monitoring workflows. Introduction to agentic tools and local models.
WEEK 08
Operating at Scale
Agentic assignment introduced: students deploy an AI agent to monitor a defined information domain over the remainder of the semester. Local models workshop: running AI without cloud infrastructure, and what that changes.
Part Two — What Does AI Make of Us? · Weeks 9–14 · Six debates on consciousness, authorship, rights, democracy, flourishing, and relationship
WEEK 09
Can AI Be Conscious?
The hard problem of consciousness. Agency as evidence of inner experience — or not. Functional equivalence versus biological requirement. If AI can be conscious, its moral status changes entirely. Students debate; AI steelmans both positions.
WEEK 10
Who Owns What AI Makes?
Authorship, intention, and judgment. Copyright law built for a different era. Training data and prior creative claim. Students produce an AI-generated creative work and argue for or against their own authorship of it.
WEEK 11
Should AI Have Rights?
Legal personhood already extends to corporations. If an AI system has agency, forms preferences, and can suffer functionally, what framework applies? Students draft and debate a policy position.
WEEK 12
Can Democracy Survive AI?
Algorithmic shaping of information environments. Deepfakes, synthetic media, personalized persuasion at scale. Regulatory problem or structural threat? Students analyze a current case using their information monitoring agents.
WEEK 13
What Is Human Flourishing When AI Does the Thinking?
Cognitive outsourcing and what remains distinctly human. Creativity, relationship, embodied experience. Students write without AI, then write the same argument with AI, and analyze the difference.
WEEK 14
Should AI Be Your Friend?
AI companions, therapists, tutors, romantic partners — not speculative, present now. What AI relationships do to human connection and the capacity for intimacy. Students assess their own AI use across the semester through this lens.
Week 15 — Synthesis and Showcase
WEEK 15
Synthesis and Showcase
Students present their information monitoring projects and semester synthesis documents. The cohort addresses the course's governing question together: what is the human future when AI is part of it? No answer required. Evidence of thinking required.

FYS 111: Living with AI

First-Year Seminar  ·  Fall Semester  ·  1 Credit  ·  AIX Explorers Section  ·  2 contact hours/week

FYS 111 is the fall first-year seminar for AIX Explorers. It runs alongside STS 190 and serves two functions: building the technical skills that sustained AI-mediated work requires, and covering the practical campus knowledge that first-year students need to navigate college. This is the first week of college. The course does not front-load theory. It builds the skills and habits students need to do the work in front of them.

Goals

1
Develop the technical skills required for agentic AI operation, information monitoring, and local model use
2
Build the practical campus literacy that first-year students need: time management, institutional navigation, policy awareness
3
Maintain and extend the workbench as a living research infrastructure from the first day of college

Objectives

1
Deploy agentic AI tools for defined research tasks and document the decisions, failures, and results
2
Operate local AI models and articulate what running AI without cloud infrastructure changes
3
Maintain an active workbench with organized transcripts, artifacts, and synthesis documents
4
Identify approved and prohibited AI use across all current course syllabi and explain the reasoning behind each policy
5
Use the library's research infrastructure for source location, verification, and citation

Student Learning Outcomes

Students will demonstrate technical facility with agentic and local AI tools sufficient to support sustained research tasks. Students will demonstrate practical campus literacy: awareness of institutional resources, time management strategies, and the ability to read and interpret course policies — including AI use policies — across their full course load.

Weekly Arc

Weeks 1–4 — Campus Foundations
WEEK 01
First Week of College
Welcome to campus. What is AIX? What is this cohort? Students set up workbenches, create cloud repository folders, and access their first AI model. Introduction to the Learner's Permit scaffold. No prior experience assumed.
WEEK 02
Mapping the Semester
All courses, all major deadlines, all assessment types laid out. Time management as infrastructure, not motivation. Where does AI help? Where does it create friction?
WEEK 03
Reading Every Syllabus
Students bring all fall syllabi. Line by line: what AI use is permitted, prohibited, or unaddressed in each course? Students build a personal AI policy map for the semester and identify where policies are unclear or contradictory.
WEEK 04
The Library
Research databases, source verification, interlibrary loan, citation tools. How do these complement AI-assisted research rather than duplicate it? Librarian-led.
Weeks 5–8 — AI Skills
WEEK 05
Agentic Tools: Introduction
What is an agent? How does agentic AI differ from generative AI? Students set up a first monitoring task: defining a domain, setting parameters, running an agent, reviewing output.
WEEK 06
Agentic Tools: Iteration
Students report on their first monitoring runs. What worked? What did the agent miss or misframe? Iteration and failure documentation. How do you know when to trust the agent and when to override it?
WEEK 07
Local Models
What changes when AI runs locally, without cloud infrastructure? Setup and first use of a local model. Comparison with cloud-based models: capability, speed, privacy, cost. Why does this matter for the data arguments in STS 190?
WEEK 08
Working at Scale
Using AI to process a corpus larger than you could read manually. Students build a small corpus and prompt a model to synthesize, identify patterns, and flag gaps. Evaluation: what did it get right?
Weeks 9–15 — Practice, Reflection, Showcase
WEEK 09
Synthesis Document Workshop
Students generate or update a synthesis document from their work to date. Peer review: does the synthesis serve as useful opening context for a new conversation? What does it miss?
WEEK 10
Verification and Citation
How do you check AI-generated citations? When is manual verification necessary and when is informed stewardship sufficient? Practice session with current coursework.
WEEK 11
Failure Documentation
Each student presents one significant AI failure from the semester: what happened, what it cost, what it revealed. What does a responsible AI practitioner do differently after a failure?
WEEK 12
The No-AI Session
One task completed without AI, in class. Students write a short reflection: what was different? What did they notice about their own thinking? What would they use AI for if they could do it again?
WEEK 13
Explaining AI to a Skeptic
Students prepare and deliver a two-minute explanation of their AI practice to a skeptic. Partners play the skeptic. Debrief: what was hardest to explain? Where did the explanation break down?
WEEK 14
Showcase Preparation
Students prepare their contribution to the end-of-semester AIX Explorers showcase: one artifact, one transcript, one reflection. What do you want to show? What does it demonstrate?
WEEK 15
Reflection and Close
What changed between August and now? Students write a final reflection on their AI practice across the semester. Workbenches reviewed. Provisional License confirmed. Next steps in the credential stack described.
