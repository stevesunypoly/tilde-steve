What We Built in Phase 1

Fall 2025 — complete

01
Policy Framework Architecture
Designed the seven-dimension values-calibration system. Three framing dimensions operate as global constraints. Four operational dimensions drive policy option generation — 128 unique combinations per policy item.
02
Four Governance Philosophies
Defined four approaches to AI policy — Secure Adoption, Risk-Limited Use, Open Use, Rights-First Governance — giving county leadership a vocabulary for how different value priorities produce different policy language.
03
Policy Explorer Design
Specified the interactive system that presents policy options through a navigation interface. Staff adjust value settings and see how adjustments change policy language. Runs entirely on county equipment and networks.
04
Environmental Scan
Established methodology for comprehensive coverage of AI and cybersecurity policy activity at the county level across the United States. Initial scan underway, providing national context for the county's own policy decisions.
05
County Operations Mapping
Began identifying where AI tools could engage with Herkimer County operations. Developed initial use cases grounded in actual county contexts, including law enforcement field operations and DSS service delivery.
06
Cybersecurity Integration
Mapped county cybersecurity posture against the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. Security is integrated as a primary operational dimension so cybersecurity is embedded in every policy option.

How Phase 2 Works

01 — IngestionCounty operational documents — policy handbooks, job descriptions, departmental procedures, forms — processed into a structured knowledge base. County documents remain on county equipment and networks throughout.
02 — Policy OutlineFrom ingested materials, we define the full set of policy topics organized by department, role, and function. This becomes the table of contents for the comprehensive policy document.
03 — Policy GenerationFor each topic, we generate a complete set of policy options across the seven dimensions — 128 alternatives per item, each reflecting different value priorities, ready for county review.
04 — Policy ExplorerThe interactive system is built and populated with generated policy text. Staff adjust value settings and see how those adjustments change policy language for any topic in real time.
05 — ValidationCounty staff review generated policy text against their operational knowledge. Quality assurance and training: staff build familiarity with the AI and cybersecurity landscape they will be governing.
06 — WorkshopsFour 2.5-hour facilitated sessions — June, July, September, October — where county leadership and staff deliberate using the Explorer and converge on a recommended policy package.
07 — AdoptionThe county administrator presents the recommended policy to the County Legislature. Target adoption: November 4, 2026.

The Framing Dimensions

Three global constraints — set once, applied to every policy item. These establish the ethical context within which all policy options are generated.

Equity
Equity
AI tools and policies are transparent in how they treat employees, residents, service providers, and recipients — and in the assumptions embedded in that treatment.
Hi: Equity is an active constraint — all AI tools are reviewed for differential impact before deployment; treatment assumptions are documented and audited.
Neutral: AI tools and policies should not systematically disadvantage any group; differential treatment is unintentional and addressed when identified.
Lo: Equity is a goal but not a procedural requirement; concerns are addressed when raised.
Human Impact
Human Impact
The effects of AI-assisted decisions on individuals and families remain visible and subject to human review.
Hi: No AI-assisted decision affecting an individual is final without human review; every determination is documented and appealable.
Neutral: AI-assisted decisions affecting individuals remain subject to human oversight where practicable.
Lo: Human review is available for AI-assisted decisions but not required as a standard step.
Infrastructure Impact
Infrastructure Impact
The material costs of AI adoption — energy use, data infrastructure, procurement, sustainability — are recognized as relevant to county operations and policy.
Hi: Energy use, data infrastructure costs, vendor dependency, and sustainability are formally evaluated as criteria before any AI tool is adopted.
Neutral: Material costs of AI adoption are considered in procurement planning alongside other operational factors.
Lo: Infrastructure costs are tracked but do not constitute a barrier to adoption decisions.

The Operational Dimensions

Four dimensions, each at Hi or Lo — 16 combinations per policy item. With three framing postures applied, 128 unique policy options per item.

Security
Security
Protecting county systems, data, and residents from harm or exposure.
Hi: approved, vetted tools; data stays on county networks.
Lo: any available tool permitted.
Efficiency
Efficiency
Reducing friction and cost in how county work gets done.
Hi: act on AI output directly; no extra approval step.
Lo: supervisor sign-off required before use.
Innovation
Innovation
Encouraging staff to use emerging tools that improve county services.
Hi: staff encouraged to experiment with new tools.
Lo: one county-designated tool only.
Accountability
Accountability
Clear human responsibility for AI-assisted decisions.
Hi: every use logged — staff ID, tool, timestamp, 90 days.
Lo: no logging required.

What We Need from the County

Four asks. SUNY Poly handles framework design, policy generation, facilitation, and delivery.

1
Operational Materials
Digital copies of policy handbooks, employee handbooks, job descriptions, departmental procedures, and forms — at whatever scope the county determines is appropriate. These feed the Policy Explorer and ensure policy language reflects how Herkimer County actually operates. County documents remain on county equipment and networks throughout.
2
Staff Participation in Validation
Designated windows where county staff review generated policy text against their operational knowledge. Quality assurance and training: staff build familiarity with the AI and cybersecurity landscape by working through the policy options.
3
Workshop Attendance
Participation from county leadership, department heads, and designated staff across the four workshops. Workshops 2 and 3 include focused training tracks organized by department or function as determined by the county.
4
Neighboring County Outreach
The county administrator has agreed to extend an invitation to peer counties for potential participation. The project methodology is designed for regional transferability, and neighboring county participation enriches the process for all parties.

The Claim

AI governance policy is not a document problem. It is a system design problem. This project builds the system — 128 pre-generated policy options per item, navigable by four operational dimensions, constrained by three framing values — deploys it in a live governance context, and produces a comprehensive policy framework ready for Herkimer County legislative adoption.
