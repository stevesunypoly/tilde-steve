Living with AI

Conversation Prep — Guest: Dr. Dana Gavin
Generative AI and the Future of the Humanities: Reading, Writing, Teaching, Labor
Palgrave Macmillan, 2025  ·  116 pp.
Dana J. Gavin, PhD — Director, Writing Center, Dutchess Community College, SUNY

Gavin's Argument — 7 Points

Her words, her logic — before critique.

  • Writing is cognitive struggle made visible. "Each word, sentence, paragraph, and composition represents a new opportunity to try one's hand at meaning-making that can be reviewed." The writer produces "a record of thought development." That record is the literacy event. (p. 25)
  • The struggle is the point, not the product. "Writing as a means to understand what we know… is necessarily challenging work, which is why student writers often only realize the thesis of their paper at the very end." Remove the struggle, lose the learning. (p. 25)
  • Writing is communal, never solitary — but accountable. She demolishes the "lone genius" myth. Writing is always an assemblage of influences and communities. But that assemblage is citable, traceable, transparent. Attribution is how the assemblage stays honest. (pp. 30–32)
  • Reading requires trust in good faith authorship. "Reading requires a level of trust that the author is acting in good faith, yet generative AI is untrustworthy." The trust circuit is what enables meaning-making to begin. (Ch. 3 abstract, p. 43)
  • AI text mimics trustworthy form without the substance. LLMs "generate new content based on patterns in existing content, and build text by predicting most likely words" — no understanding, no context, no intent. It looks like knowledge. It isn't. (p. 39)
  • The epistemic commons is already corroding — AI accelerates it. "It is not hyperbolic to suggest that we are at the crisis' edge in terms of critical thinking and information literacy. Generative AI equips bad actors with accessible tools." (p. 22)
  • The way forward is interrogation, not avoidance. "We must interrogate AI-generated content so that we can explain to students what this tool is… deep reading and writing processes are key — let us lean into that process as a method of interrogation." (p. 22)

The Punch Opener

For Chris — opens everything:

"Dr. Gavin — two-part question. How should a first-year composition student be using ChatGPT? And what should that student absolutely not do — not because it's cheating, but because it would rob them of the very cognitive struggle that makes them a better thinker and writer?"

Her answer will reveal whether the line is at AI use or passive AI use. Either way, the conversation opens into the heart of it.

Three Drill-Down Questions

Q1 — The real disagreement
"The AI isn't untrustworthy — it's the way we use AI. Your writing-is-assemblage argument means no author was ever the sole origin of a text. What you're actually defending isn't authorial purity — it's the accountability infrastructure: citation, attribution, traceability. A transparent prompt archive restores that. So is the problem AI, or bad use of AI?"
Q2 — The fault line
"You trace the same anxiety — and the same instinct toward avoidance — from the steam press through Google to now. It never worked. The commons got flooded anyway. Why is avoidance the right call this time?"
Q3 — Where we agree
"The bypass isn't in the AI — it's in passive consumption of the response. If we require students to commit to a position before prompting, predict what the AI will say, then write their reaction — isn't that more cognitive struggle than a traditional essay where the process is invisible? Where does the struggle end and the bypass begin — and isn't that a pedagogy problem, not an AI problem?"

The Core Argument — 7 Points

Where we push back — and where we land together.

  • Her diagnosis requires our prescription. If the epistemic commons is flooded with authoritative-sounding unaccountable text — you can't drain it. The only solution that scales is readers who know what they're doing, whatever the source.
  • The bypass is passive consumption, not AI use. Speed isn't the problem. Frequency isn't the problem. The bypass happens when a student accepts the output without cognitive engagement — the same way they scroll Instagram. That's a literacy problem, not a technology problem.
  • The prompt is the cognitive struggle. Five words or five hundred — if you're formulating a question, committing to a position, evaluating a response, you're doing the work. The question is whether we design that interaction to demand the struggle.
  • The goal of the struggle is self-knowledge. Gavin: the thesis appears at the end of the paper because writing is how you find out what you believe. Neural activation is not a lower bar. It may be a higher one — because the archive doesn't lie about whether thinking happened.
  • Avoiding AI doesn't protect students — it sends them in unarmed. A student who has never practiced critical engagement with AI-generated text is more vulnerable to it, not less. The learner's permit precedes the commercial license.
  • Her pedagogical prescriptions don't require avoiding AI. Process-oriented, scaffolded, reflective, low-stakes — everything she calls for in Chapter 5 is fully compatible with AI-integrated practice. She's describing the method. We're applying it to the medium.
  • Design is the answer. Require commitment before response. Require prediction. Require written reaction. Require documentation. The struggle is in the gap between what you thought and what came back. The prompt is a wager — and the archive is the proof.
