Early AI systems could perform impressively in narrow, formal domains such as chess or logical inference, but they lacked the background knowledge humans use effortlessly in everyday reasoning. Alan Turing’s imitation game, later called the Turing Test, proposed that a machine should be considered intelligent if it could converse indistinguishably from a human. Words like _weight_, _balance_, or _danger_ are tied to sensorimotor interactions with the environment.

Symbolic AI, by contrast, operated largely in abstract formal spaces.

This concern eventually crystallized into what became known as the **symbol grounding problem**. If every symbol in a system is defined only in terms of other symbols, the system risks becoming a closed interpretive loop—an internally consistent language game with no contact with reality.

The third challenge was **the experience problem**.

Some philosophers argued that intelligence may not be reducible to behavioral competence alone. Human cognition involves subjective experience—what philosophers call _phenomenology_. Thomas Nagel’s famous question, “What is it like to be a bat?” and later David Chalmers’ formulation of the _hard problem of consciousness_ pushed the debate into deeper territory.

Could a machine simulate intelligent behavior without possessing any inner life at all?

For decades these critiques shaped the philosophical landscape surrounding AI.

But the arrival of large-scale machine learning systems—especially large language models—has complicated this picture in a surprising way.

Not because they solve the philosophical problems.

But because they change where those problems apply.

Edwin Hutchins, studying navigation teams on naval vessels, observed that complex reasoning often emerges not from an individual mind but from the interaction between people, tools, and external representations. What emerges is something closer to a **hybrid epistemic system**.

In this system, intelligent behavior arises from the interaction between three components: human cultural knowledge embedded in training data, machine learning architectures that compress and recombine that knowledge, and human participants who actively steer and evaluate the outputs.

From this perspective, large language models are not simply artificial minds. They may be better understood as **cognitive infrastructure**—machines that participate in distributed reasoning processes spanning both humans and machines.

Importantly, this interpretation does not dissolve the classical objections.

A critic like Searle could still argue that the machine itself performs only syntactic transformations. The semantics—the meaning—still resides with the human interpreters.

Instead of asking whether the machine alone understands, we might ask whether **human–machine networks can collectively produce forms of reasoning that neither component could achieve independently**.

If that is correct, then the philosophical challenge shifts.

The central question is no longer simply whether machines can think.

The deeper question is whether intelligence itself may increasingly emerge from **complex socio-technical systems that integrate biological cognition with machine inference**.

Understanding artificial intelligence, in that case, may require expanding philosophy of mind into something broader: a philosophy of **hybrid cognition**.
